Exploring what shapes life satisfaction around the world, using the World Happiness Report

What are the drivers of happiness?

Why it matters: understanding the relative importance of economic, societal, institutional, and technological factors in citizens' happiness and well-being could help policymakers create more effective legislation.

Introduction

The World Happiness Report (WHR) is published by the University of Oxford's Wellbeing Research Centre in partnership with Gallup and the UN Sustainable Development Solutions Network. Its underlying data comes from the Gallup World Poll. It measures happiness using the following question, which refers to Cantril's ladder:

"Please imagine a ladder with steps numbered from 0 at the bottom to 10 at the top. The top of the ladder represents the best possible life for you and the bottom of the ladder represents the worst possible life for you. On which step of the ladder would you say you personally feel you stand at this time?"

The WHR team has been measuring happiness for 20 years (2006-2025), and has found that six variables account for more than three-quarters of the variation in how people answer this question:

  • Having someone to count on for help
  • GDP per capita
  • Healthy life expectancy
  • Freedom to make life choices
  • Generosity
  • Freedom from corruption in government or business

In the WHR data, these variables are correlated with higher numbers on Cantril's ladder. However, the WHR data cannot prove causality.

Task 1

Quickly identify the most and least happy countries

Our first visualization is a baseline choropleth map of global happiness, starting in 2006. We wanted to begin with the simplest question: where are countries happier?

Color encodes the life evaluation score, where greener countries score higher and redder countries score lower. This gives a quick global overview and makes geographic patterns easier to notice. Instead of scanning a table of scores, you can immediately see regional clusters. Some regions run consistently higher or lower in happiness, which leads into the next question of why those differences might exist.

At this stage, the map is mostly descriptive. It does not explain the causes yet, but it gives us a starting point for asking better questions.

Task 2

Compare countries and understand the factors underlying their life satisfaction scores

Each line shows a country's average life evaluation at two points in time, comparing the 2006-2008 average with the 2023-2025 average so that one unusual survey year cannot drive the ranking.

The biggest gains cluster in Eastern Europe, Central Asia, and Latin America, with countries like Georgia, Kosovo, and Latvia climbing about a point and a half as their economies and institutions matured. The steepest declines include countries under visible economic or political strain such as Lebanon, Egypt, and Venezuela, which suggests happiness responds to stability and governance as much as to income.

Task 5

Identify examples of positive and negative deviance

Gallup's model of happiness -- having someone to count on, sufficient GDP per capita, healthy life expectancy, freedom to make life choices, generosity, and freedom from corruption -- allows us to predict the Cantril ladder score for a specific country, given the values for these other variables. The visualizations below show the degree to which a country is above or below its predicted happiness. It averages this positive or negative deviance from predictions over the years selected on the right. Green indicates positive deviance and red indicates negative deviance.

The 'Select View' option on the top right allows toggling between a map and a ranked list of countries by deviance (positive to negative). Countries that are grey (and have no pop-up when you hover over them) did not have sufficient data in the variables listed above to calculate their predicted happiness levels.

Task 6

Understand what drives positive and negative deviance

In the visualization below, the top chart plots the five countries that most exceed their predicted happiness and the five that most fall short of theirs. The data is averaged over the years selected. When you click on a country bar in the top chart, the two charts below focus on that country. The middle chart compares the ways that the country most exceeds (or falls below) their income group peers globally. The bottom chart does the same for the country's regional peers.

In these charts, we include variables that go beyond the standard WHR six (see above), such as measures of:

  • Governance. Voice and accountability, control of corruption, rule of law, political stability, government effectiveness, regulatory quality, mean governance (averages the preceding six), and mean government delivery (averages control of corruption, rule of law, government effectiveness, and regulatory quality).
  • Human development. Participation of women in parliament, years of education for men and women (expected and actual), and labor force participation by men and women.
  • Financial equality. Share of national income held by the bottom 50% or middle 40%.
  • Emotions. Experience enjoyment yesterday, positive affect (averages measures of smiling, enjoying, and doing something interesting yesterday).

In addition, we standardized the variable values so that measures with very different units (such as GDP per capita and the percent of people who smiled yesterday) can be compared on a common scale. The x axis in the bottom two charts shows how far the selected country is from its peer-group average, measured in standard deviations for each variable. So if a country is 1.8 standard deviations above its peer average, it is nearly twice the average difference from the group average with respect to that variable.

The charts capture the best five variables for countries with positive deviance and the worst five variables for countries with negative deviance. However, if there are less than five advantages or disadvantages, the chart will show a mix of advantages and disadvantages.
