Introduction

The World Happiness Report (WHR) is published by the University of Oxford's Wellbeing Research Centre in partnership with Gallup and the UN Sustainable Development Solutions Network. Its underlying data comes from the Gallup World Poll. It measures happiness using the following question, which refers to Cantril's ladder:

"Please imagine a ladder with steps numbered from 0 at the bottom to 10 at the top. The top of the ladder represents the best possible life for you and the bottom of the ladder represents the worst possible life for you. On which step of the ladder would you say you personally feel you stand at this time?"

The WHR team has been measuring happiness for 20 years (2006-2025), and has found that six variables account for more than three-quarters of the variation in how people answer this question:

  • Having someone to count on for help (also called social support)
  • GDP per capita
  • Healthy life expectancy
  • Freedom to make life choices
  • Generosity
  • Freedom from corruption in government or business

In the WHR data, these variables are correlated with higher numbers on Cantril's ladder. However, the WHR data cannot prove causality.

Below, we look at these six WHR variables, additional variables from the Gallup World Poll, and variables from the following:

  • Human Development Index. These include measures of the participation of women in parliament, years of education for men and women (expected and actual), and labor force participation by men and women. This source also provides measures of gender inequality and inequality in healthy life expectancy. The Human Development Index itself is a blend of healthy life expectancy, education, and GDP per capita.
  • World Inequality Database. These include the share of a nation's income held by the bottom 50% and the middle 40% (versus the top 1% or 10%).
  • World Governance Indicators. These include measures of democracy (voice and accountability), political stability, and government effectiveness (regulatory quality, rule of law, control of corruption, and government effectiveness). The mean governance score averages these six measures, and the mean government delivery score averages the four elements of government effectiveness.

Many of these variables are correlated with the WHR six. For example, government effectiveness is highly correlated with GDP per capita and healthy life expectancy, and moderately correlated with the freedom to make choices. We hypothesize that some of these additional variables may play a role in shaping the WHR six.

Task 1

Quickly identify the most and least happy countries

Our first visualization is a baseline choropleth map of global happiness, starting in 2006. We wanted to begin with the simplest question: where are countries happier?

Color encodes the life evaluation score, where greener countries score higher and redder countries score lower. This gives a quick global overview and makes geographic patterns easier to notice. Instead of scanning a table of scores, you can immediately see regional clusters. Some regions run consistently higher or lower in happiness, which leads into the next question of why those differences might exist.

At this stage, the map is mostly descriptive. It does not explain the causes yet, but it gives us a starting point for asking better questions.

Task 2

Compare countries and understand the factors underlying their life satisfaction scores

Each line shows a country's average life evaluation at two points in time, comparing the 2006-2008 average with the 2023-2025 average so that one unusual survey year cannot drive the ranking.

The biggest gains cluster in Eastern Europe, Central Asia, and Latin America, with countries like Georgia, Kosovo, and Latvia climbing about a point and a half as their economies and institutions matured. The steepest declines include countries under visible economic or political strain such as Lebanon, Egypt, and Venezuela, which suggests happiness responds to stability and governance as much as to income.

Task 5

Identify examples of positive and negative deviance

Gallup's model of happiness -- having someone to count on, sufficient GDP per capita, healthy life expectancy, freedom to make life choices, generosity, and freedom from corruption -- allows us to predict the Cantril ladder score for a specific country, given the values for these other variables. The visualizations below show the degree to which a country is above or below its predicted happiness. It averages this positive or negative deviance over the years selected on the right. Green indicates degrees of positive deviance and red indicates degrees of negative deviance.

The 'Select View' option on the top right allows toggling between this map and a ranked list of countries by deviance (positive to negative). Countries that are grey (and have no pop-up when you hover over them) did not have sufficient data in the variables listed above to calculate their predicted happiness levels.

Task 6

Understand what drives positive and negative deviance

In the visualization below, the top chart plots the five countries that most exceed their predicted happiness and the five that most fall short of theirs. The data is averaged over the years selected. When you click on a country bar in the top chart, the two charts below focus on that country. The middle chart compares the ways that the country most exceeds (or falls below) their income group peers globally. The bottom chart does the same for the country's regional peers. The charts capture the best five variables for countries with positive deviance and the worst five variables for countries with negative deviance.

In these charts, we standardized the variable values so that measures with very different units (such as GDP per capita and the percent of people who smiled yesterday) can be compared on a common scale. The x axis in the bottom two charts shows how far the selected country is from its peer-group average, measured in standard deviations for each variable. So if a country is 1.8 standard deviations above its peer average, it is nearly twice the average difference from the group average with respect to that variable.

Notes on selected variables:

  • Coefficient of human inequality. This is an average of inequality in health, education, and income.
  • Corruption perception. This variable is the average of those answering in the affirmative to these two questions: (1) "Is corruption widespread throughout the government or not" (2) "Is corruption widespread within businesses or not?"
  • GDP per capita. This measures a country's economic output per person. Values are expressed in constant 2021 "international dollars," where one international dollar has approximately the same purchasing power as one U.S. dollar had in the United States in 2021. This data comes from the World Bank's World Development Indicators.
  • Gender inequality index. This measure incorporates reproductive health, share of parliamentary seats held by women, the percentage of women with at least some secondary education, and women's labor force participation.
  • Generosity. Generosity uses GDP per capita to establish a predicted number for individual monetary donations (measured by the question "Have you donated money to a charity in the past month?"). A negative number means that a country's citizens are less generous that predicted by its GDP per capita and a positive number means that its citizens are more generous than predicted.
  • Healthy life expectancy. Healthy life expectancies at birth is the expected number of years a person in a given country will remain healthy.
  • Mean governance score. This is an average of the six governance measures: voice and accountability, control of corruption, rule of law, political stability, government effectiveness, and regulatory quality.
  • Mean government delivery score. This is an average of four of the governance measures: control of corruption, rule of law, government effectiveness, and regulatory quality
  • Negative affect. This is an average of the following questions (when available): (1) "Did you experience worry during A LOT OF THE DAY yesterday??" (2) "Did you experience sadness during A LOT OF THE DAY yesterday?" (3) "Did you experience anger during A LOT OF THE DAY yesterday?"
  • Negative experience index. This is an average of the following questions (when available): (1) "Did you experience physical pain during a lot of the day yesterday?" (2) "Did you experience stress during a lot of the day yesterday?" (3) "Did you experience worry during A LOT OF THE DAY yesterday??" (4) "Did you experience sadness during A LOT OF THE DAY yesterday?" (5) "Did you experience anger during A LOT OF THE DAY yesterday?"
  • Positive affect. This is an average of the following questions (when available): (1) "Did you smile or laugh a lot yesterday?" (2) "Did you experience enjoyment during A LOT OF THE DAY yesterday?" (3) "“Did you learn or do something interesting yesterday?"
  • Positive experience index. This is an average of the following questions (when available): (1) "Did you feel well-rested yesterday?" (2) "Were you treated with respect all day yesterday?" (3) "Did you smile or laugh a lot yesterday?" (4) "Did you learn or do something interesting yesterday?" (5) "Did you experience enjoyment during a lot of the day yesterday?"
  • Social support. Social support is a country's national average of those who answer yes to the following question: "If you were in trouble, do you have relatives or friends you can count on to help you whenever you need them, or not?"
